what_i_learned: |
  Today I learned more about the research papers we read, studied, and discussed as a group. We each went through three research papers and also looked at 
  different research related to molecular docking. One thing I learned about was a stacked ensemble model used in Ranjish’s paper to find tau inhibitors through 
  AI pipelines. It helped me understand how researchers can use multiple models together to better identify strong compounds.
  
  I also learned that our project is looking for a single model that can help us find the strongest compounds across all of our targets. We discussed how many of 
  the research papers we found were written and published in either India or America. We believe that may be because both countries have large populations and a 
  lot of research activity in this area.
  In Austin’s paper, he shared an image of curcumin. It showed that curcumin has strong potential in research, but when it was tested on patients, there was not 
  enough proof that it actually helped with memory loss. That helped me understand the difference between a compound looking promising in a model or lab setting 
  and actually being proven to work in humans.
  I also learned that the papers followed many of the same steps we are using in our own project, including QSAR and 2D-QSAR. These methods help researchers find 
  features of a compound that may be connected to its biological activity. I learned that some compounds can stay in the body too long because the body cannot 
  break them down fast enough. Other compounds may leave the body too quickly, which can make them less effective. Increasing the dose is not always an option 
  because too much of a compound can become toxic. A stronger compound is one that can be effective without being harmful to the body.
  Another thing I learned was that researchers remove descriptors that are too similar so they can focus on the most important features and top compounds. My 
  paper was focused on AChE inhibitors and how they were tracked using molecular docking and QSAR models. I now understand more about the highest-ranked compounds 
  and one major gap in the research: many of these compounds still need human testing to see whether they truly help with memory loss.
