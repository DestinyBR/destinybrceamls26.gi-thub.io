what_i_learned: |
  Today I learned more from both my own research and from hearing what each of my teammates gathered from the research papers Dr. Paudel assigned us to review. It was interesting seeing how each paper approached Alzheimer’s Disease (AD) differently but still shared similar challenges.
  I learned that, like many drugs and clinical trials, drug discovery often begins with animal models, most commonly mice. To observe if the drugs are safe before moving into patient testing. One idea that kept appearing across studies was that Alzheimer’s is incredibly complex. Researchers are not always able to clearly determine whether a drug is truly healing areas of the brain or just reducing symptoms for a temporary period of time.
  Another thing that changed my perspective was learning that Alzheimer’s is no longer viewed only as a disease connected to older age and natural wear and tear of the brain. Instead, there is growing understanding that biological imbalances and multiple contributing factors may occur much earlier than expected. Increasing the urgency and importance of early target intervention.
  My own paper had me learn more about the importance of targets and proteins in AD drug discovery. I created a breakdown explaining what each target does, how it impacts the brain, and the role of the proteins connected to Alzheimer’s research. My team then used my findings by researching datasets connected to each of one of the targets I identified.
  Lastly, I learned how important data cleaning is throughout research. While searching through PubChem to locate a dataset aligned with my selected target, NMDA, which is a receptor connected to glutamate. I understand that finding data is not enough. The data has to be cleaned, filtered, and evaluated to make sure it actually allows the ML model to predict with more ease. 


blockers: |
  There were not necessarily any blockers today. It felt more like a cycle of reading, sharing findings, researching, and repeating. The work itself was straightforward, it just required focus and patience to continuously connect information across papers, targets, and datasets. 

reflection: |
  Today made me realize that I contributed something meaningful to the group. I felt proud that my work naturally fed into the progress of our team and helped direct the next phase of research. At the same time, I felt a little underwhelmed by my assigned research paper because it was mostly a list of compounds and targets without much interpretation. I had to create value from it myself by building tables, breaking down each target, and connecting the information back to literature and biological meaning. At first that felt disappointing, but looking back, I think that process taught me more than having everything explained for me.
