what_i_learned: |

I learned more about the mistakes I made in my QSAR workflow and how small coding details can affect the whole notebook. For example, I had to pay attention to column names, missing values, and making sure my property columns were actually being read as numbers.
I learned how important data cleaning is before training a model. When I filled in missing values, especially for properties like XLogP, I started to understand that the model can only make useful predictions if the data is organized and clean first.
I learned the difference between the model’s accuracy and the model’s prediction. Accuracy tells me how well the model performed on the test data, while the prediction tells me what the model thinks about each individual compound.
I learned why my compound choices, property choices, and dataset choices matter compared to my teammates. Since everyone is choosing different compounds, we can compare which compounds look more useful for Alzheimer’s drug discovery and which properties seem to matter most.
I learned how to look at a dataset and decide which properties can be used as variables for the model. In my case, I used molecular weight, XLogP, TPSA, hydrogen bond donor count, and hydrogen bond acceptor count because they help describe drug-like behavior.
I learned how to create a prediction label from my drug discovery compounds. The label helped separate compounds into promising and not promising based on a simple teaching rule. This helped me understand how labels are important in our pipeline because the model needs something to learn from.
I learned that QSAR is not just about coding. It is about connecting chemical structure, molecular properties, and possible biological activity. I am starting to understand how this can support our larger drug discovery goal.

blockers: |  
My biggest blocker was fully understanding how to clean the data the right way, especially when values were missing or not being read correctly.
I was also confused about the difference between model accuracy and model prediction. At first, I thought they were almost the same, but now I understand that accuracy is about overall performance, while prediction is about what the model says for each compound.
Another blocker was understanding SMILES. The SMILES information looked confusing because it has symbols, numbers, and letters, but I now understand that it represents the chemical structure in text form. I am still learning whether SMILES should be used directly as a feature or mainly kept as structure information for the compound.


reflection: |
This QSAR practice helped me feel more connected to the technical side of our drug discovery project. I am starting to see how machine learning can support Alzheimer’s research by helping us organize compounds, compare properties, and rank possible candidates.
I also realized that my mistakes were part of me learning the workflow. Fixing errors with missing values, labels, and predictions helped me understand the code more instead of just running it.
I feel like I am growing in my ability to troubleshoot machine learning problems. Even though I do not fully understand every detail yet, I can now explain the basic pipeline: collect compound data, clean the properties, create a teaching label, train a model, check the accuracy, rank the compounds, and save the most promising results.
Overall, I am becoming more confident with QSAR because I understand that it is a step-by-step process. The model is not giving a final drug discovery answer yet, but it is helping us practice how to move from raw compound data toward useful predictions for our project pipeline.
